How Infections Happen

Germs (bacteria, viruses, fungi) need a way into your body, a way to travel between people, and your body has ways to block them. Think of it like a castle: attackers need a gate, a road, and defenders.

Pathways of Infection (How germs get in)

  • Cuts & wounds — A scrape on your knee lets bacteria from the ground straight into your blood.
  • Breathing — When someone with a cold sneezes, you inhale tiny droplets carrying the virus.
  • Eating / drinking — Drinking water with bacteria in it (like when camping near untreated streams) can make you sick.
Real-world example
If you step on a rusty nail at the beach, bacteria from the soil can enter through the wound. That's why doctors ask if your tetanus shot is up to date.

Routes of Transmission (How germs travel)

  • Droplet — Sneezing or coughing sends tiny droplets into the air (flu, COVID).
  • Direct contact — Shaking hands with someone who has a cold, then touching your face.
  • Contaminated food/water — Eating chicken that wasn't cooked properly can spread Salmonella.

Protection Against Infection

  • Washing hands — Soap breaks apart the outer layer of many germs.
  • Vaccination — Trains your immune system to fight specific germs before you catch them.
  • Cooking food properly — Heat kills most bacteria in meat and eggs.

Three Anatomical Barriers

Your body has built-in walls that stop germs before they even reach your cells:

  1. Skin — Your largest organ acts like armour. As long as it's not cut, most germs can't get through.
  2. Mucous membranes — Sticky mucus in your nose and throat traps germs like flypaper.
  3. Stomach acid — A pool of acid (pH ~2) that destroys most bacteria you swallow.
Think of it this way
Your body is like a medieval castle: the skin is the stone wall, mucus is the moat that traps invaders, and stomach acid is boiling oil poured on anything that gets inside.

Your Immune Army

When germs slip past your barriers, specialised cells kick into action. Each one has a specific job — like positions on a football team.

Macrophages — The First Responders ▼

Job: Macrophages patrol your body like security guards. When they find a germ, they eat it. This process is called phagocytosis.

Phagocytosis Step by Step

  1. The macrophage detects a bacterium nearby.
  2. It stretches its membrane around the bacterium, wrapping it up.
  3. The bacterium is pulled inside into a bubble (vesicle).
  4. Enzymes inside the macrophage digest and destroy the bacterium.
  5. The macrophage displays pieces of the germ on its surface like a "wanted poster" — these are called antigens.
Picture this
Imagine a Pac-Man chomping a ghost. The macrophage surrounds the germ, swallows it, dissolves it, then shows a piece of it to the rest of the immune system: "Hey, look what I found — everyone watch out for this!"
T-Helper Cells — The Commanders ▼

Job: T-Helper cells read the "wanted poster" (antigen) on the macrophage and send out chemical alarm signals to activate the rest of the immune system.

Think of it like
A football coach who spots the opponent's play and shouts instructions to the entire team. They don't fight directly — they coordinate.
T-Killer Cells — The Destroyers ▼

Job: Once activated by T-Helper cells, T-Killer cells find and destroy body cells that have already been infected by a virus. They punch holes in the infected cell's membrane.

Real example
When you get the flu, some of your throat cells get infected. T-Killer cells destroy those infected cells so the virus can't keep multiplying inside them.
Plasma Cells — The Weapon Factories ▼

Job: Plasma cells (made from B-cells) produce antibodies — Y-shaped proteins that lock onto germs and mark them for destruction. One plasma cell can release thousands of antibodies per second.

Analogy
Antibodies are like sticky labels that stick to germs saying "DESTROY ME." Once labelled, macrophages can find and eat them much faster.
Memory Cells — The Record Keepers ▼

Job: After you recover, Memory cells remember exactly what that germ looked like. If the same germ comes back years later, they trigger a fast, powerful response — so fast you might not even feel sick.

That's why
You usually only get chickenpox once. Your memory cells "remember" the virus and crush it immediately if it shows up again.

The Specific Immune Response — Full Sequence

Vaccinations

Active Immunisation

Your body is given a weakened or dead form of the germ (or a piece of it). Your immune system fights it, makes antibodies, and — most importantly — creates memory cells. This gives long-lasting protection.

Example
The MMR vaccine (measles, mumps, rubella) is given to children around age 1. It contains weakened viruses. Your body builds memory cells that protect you for life.

Passive Immunisation

You receive ready-made antibodies from another source. These fight the infection immediately, but they don't last — your body didn't learn to make them itself, so no memory cells are formed.

Example
If someone is bitten by a venomous snake, they get an antivenom injection containing pre-made antibodies. It works fast but only temporarily.

Advantages of Vaccination

  1. Prevents deadly diseases (smallpox was wiped out entirely by vaccination).
  2. Protects people who can't be vaccinated (babies, people with weak immune systems) through herd immunity.
  3. Cheaper than treating the disease itself — prevents expensive hospital stays.

Disadvantages / Concerns

  1. Mild side effects are common (sore arm, low fever for a day).
  2. Some vaccines need multiple doses or boosters, which can be inconvenient.
  3. Rare allergic reactions can occur in some individuals.

Allergies

What is an Allergen?

An allergen is a normally harmless substance that the immune system mistakenly treats as dangerous. Your body overreacts to something that isn't actually a threat.

Common allergens
Pollen (from flowers and trees), peanuts (in food), and dust mites (tiny creatures living in pillows and carpets).

What Happens During an Allergic Reaction

  1. First exposure: Your body meets the allergen (e.g., pollen) and mistakenly decides it's harmful. Plasma cells produce special antibodies (IgE) for that allergen.
  2. Antibodies attach to mast cells (found in your skin, nose, and lungs) and wait.
  3. Second exposure: The allergen returns. It binds to the antibodies already sitting on the mast cells.
  4. Mast cells burst open and release histamine.
  5. Histamine causes the symptoms: sneezing, itchy eyes, swelling, rash, or in severe cases, difficulty breathing.
Key point
The first time you encounter an allergen, you usually don't react. It's the second time (or later) that your body overreacts, because it already has antibodies waiting.

How Can Allergies Be Treated?

  1. Antihistamines — Medicines that block histamine from causing symptoms. Example: taking a pill before pollen season to stop sneezing.
  2. Desensitisation (allergy shots) — Small, increasing doses of the allergen are injected over months/years. This trains the immune system to stop overreacting.

Habitats & Adaptations

Key Definitions

  • Habitat — The specific place where an organism lives (e.g., a pond, a rainforest canopy, under a rock).
  • Ecosystem — A habitat plus all the living things in it AND the non-living things (water, temperature, soil) that interact together.
  • Biosphere — All the ecosystems on Earth combined — every living thing and its environment.
  • Ecology — The science of studying how living things interact with each other and their environment.
  • Adaptation — A feature (body part, behaviour, or internal process) that helps an organism survive in its habitat.

Three Examples of Animal Adaptations

Structural
Polar bears have thick white fur and a layer of fat (blubber). The fur provides camouflage in snow, and the fat insulates against freezing temperatures.
Behavioural
Arctic foxes curl into a tight ball and wrap their bushy tail over their nose when sleeping to reduce heat loss.
Physiological
Camels can drink up to 100 litres of water in 10 minutes and store fat in their hump, allowing them to survive days without water in the desert.

Daily and Seasonal Changes

Daily: Temperature drops at night. Nocturnal animals like owls have adapted with large eyes to see in low light and hunt at night when their prey (mice) is active.

Seasonal: In winter, food becomes scarce and temperatures plummet. Hedgehogs hibernate — they slow their heart rate and body temperature to save energy until spring.

Food Webs

Key Definitions

  • Population — All the individuals of one species living in the same area (e.g., all the rabbits in a meadow).
  • Producer — An organism that makes its own food using sunlight (photosynthesis). Example: grass, oak trees, algae.
  • Consumer — An organism that eats other organisms. Example: rabbits eat grass, hawks eat rabbits.
  • Decomposer — An organism that breaks down dead matter and recycles nutrients back into the soil. Example: fungi, earthworms, bacteria.
  • Food chain — A single pathway showing what eats what: Grass → Rabbit → Fox.
  • Food web — Many food chains linked together, showing the full network of who eats whom in an ecosystem.

What Happens if You Remove a Species?

Example — meadow food web
If all the frogs disappeared from a pond ecosystem: insects (which frogs eat) would multiply rapidly, damaging crops and plants. Meanwhile, herons (which eat frogs) would lose a food source, and their population would decline. The whole web shifts.

Invasive Species

An invasive species is a non-native organism introduced to an ecosystem where it has no natural predators. It can outcompete native species for food and space, causing populations to crash.

Real example
Cane toads were introduced to Australia in 1935 to control beetles. With no natural predators, their population exploded. They poison animals that try to eat them and compete with native frogs for food.

Three Ways Humans Negatively Impact Ecosystems

  1. Deforestation — Cutting down forests destroys habitats. Orangutans in Borneo are critically endangered because their rainforest home is being cleared for palm oil farms.
  2. Pollution — Plastic waste in the ocean is eaten by sea turtles who mistake it for jellyfish, often killing them.
  3. Overfishing — Taking too many fish (like Atlantic cod) faster than they can reproduce crashes their population and disrupts the entire marine food web.

Flow of Energy

Consumer Levels

  • Primary consumer — Eats producers. Example: a grasshopper eating grass.
  • Secondary consumer — Eats primary consumers. Example: a frog eating the grasshopper.
  • Tertiary consumer — Eats secondary consumers. Example: a hawk eating the frog.

The Energy Pyramid

Energy decreases as you move up each level. At every step, about 90% of energy is lost (as heat from movement, body warmth, and life processes). Only ~10% passes to the next level.

Each level keeps only ~10% of the energy from the level below it.

Calculating Energy Flow

Worked example
A field of grass captures 100,000 kJ of energy from the sun.

Grasshoppers (primary consumers) get 10% → 10,000 kJ
Frogs (secondary consumers) get 10% of that → 1,000 kJ
Hawks (tertiary consumers) get 10% of that → 100 kJ

Formula: Energy at next level = Energy at current level × 0.10

Biomass Also Decreases

Biomass is the total mass of living organisms at each level. Like energy, biomass gets smaller at higher levels. There are far more blades of grass (by mass) than hawks. This is why top predators are rare — there simply isn't enough energy to support many of them.
