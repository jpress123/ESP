Your body is like a castle. Germs are the invaders trying to break in. They need a way in, a road to travel, and your body has walls to block them.

Infections
Your Body's Three Walls

Before germs even reach your cells, your body has anatomical barriers — built-in defences that work automatically.

🧹

Skin

Your largest organ. Like armour — as long as it's not broken, most germs can't pass through.

🧻

Mucous Membranes

Sticky mucus in your nose and throat traps germs — like flypaper catching flies.

🧪

Stomach Acid

A pool of powerful acid (pH ~2) that dissolves most bacteria you accidentally swallow.

Immune Response
Macrophages — The First Responders

When germs slip past the barriers, macrophages are first on the scene. They patrol your body like security guards — and they eat germs alive.

Phagocytosis (How macrophages eat germs)

1
Macrophage detects a bacterium nearby
2
Stretches its membrane and surrounds the bacterium
3
Pulls it inside into a bubble (vesicle)
4
Enzymes digest and destroy the bacterium
5
Displays antigen on its surface — a "wanted poster" for other immune cells
Immune Response
Your Immune Cell Team

Each cell type has a unique role — like positions on a football team.

T-Helper Cells — The Commanders

Read the antigen on the macrophage and send alarm signals to activate the rest of the immune system. They coordinate, not fight.

T-Killer Cells — The Destroyers

Find body cells already infected by a virus and punch holes in them to destroy the virus factory.

Plasma Cells — Weapon Factories

Produce antibodies — Y-shaped proteins that stick to germs like labels saying "DESTROY ME."

Memory Cells — Record Keepers

Stay in your body for years. If the same germ returns, they trigger a lightning-fast response. That's why you only get chickenpox once.

Vaccinations
Active vs. Passive Immunisation

Active Immunisation

You receive a weakened or dead germ. Your body fights it, builds antibodies, and creates memory cells.

Example: The MMR vaccine — given around age 1. Protects for life.

Slow to work (days) but long-lasting.

Passive Immunisation

You receive ready-made antibodies from another source. Works immediately but your body doesn't learn — no memory cells.

Example: Snake antivenom — pre-made antibodies injected after a bite.

Fast but temporary.

Vaccinations
Advantages & Disadvantages

Advantages

  1. Prevents deadly diseases — smallpox was wiped out entirely by vaccines
  2. Herd immunity protects babies and people with weak immune systems
  3. Cheaper than treating the disease — fewer hospital stays

Disadvantages

  1. Mild side effects are common (sore arm, low fever for a day)
  2. Multiple doses or boosters may be needed
  3. Rare allergic reactions can occur in some people
Allergies
What Is an Allergen?

An allergen is a normally harmless substance that your immune system mistakenly treats as dangerous.

🌼

Pollen

From flowers, grass, and trees. Causes hay fever in spring.

🥜

Peanuts

A food allergen that can cause severe reactions in some people.

🐛

Dust Mites

Tiny creatures in pillows and carpets. Can trigger sneezing and asthma.

Allergies
What Happens During an Allergic Reaction?
1
First exposure: Body meets allergen (e.g., pollen). Plasma cells produce special IgE antibodies.
2
Antibodies attach to mast cells (in skin, nose, lungs) and wait.
3
Second exposure: Allergen returns and binds to the antibodies on mast cells.
4
Mast cells burst open and release histamine.
5
Histamine causes symptoms: sneezing, swelling, itchy eyes, rash, difficulty breathing.
Allergies
Treating Allergies

Antihistamines

Medicines that block histamine from causing symptoms.

Example: Taking a tablet before pollen season to stop sneezing and itchy eyes.

Desensitisation (Allergy Shots)

Small, increasing doses of the allergen are injected over months or years.

Trains the immune system to stop overreacting. Takes time but can provide lasting relief.

Key Point

The first time you meet an allergen, you usually don't react. The second time is when your body overreacts — because antibodies are already waiting on the mast cells.

Topic 4 • Basic Ecology
Habitats & Key Definitions
🌎
Habitat — The specific place where an organism lives (a pond, a tree canopy, under a rock).
🌳
Ecosystem — A habitat + all living things + non-living things (water, soil, temperature) interacting together.
🌐
Biosphere — All ecosystems on Earth combined. Every living thing and its environment.
🔎
Ecology — The science of studying how living things interact with each other and their environment.
⚡
Adaptation — A feature (body part, behaviour, or process) that helps an organism survive in its habitat.

Habitats
Daily & Seasonal Changes

Daily Change

Temperature drops at night.

Adaptation: Owls have huge eyes to see in low light. They hunt at night when mice are active — avoiding competition with daytime hunters.

Seasonal Change

In winter, food becomes scarce and temperatures plummet.

Adaptation: Hedgehogs hibernate — slowing heart rate and body temperature to save energy until spring returns.

Food Webs
Key Definitions
👥
Population — All individuals of one species in the same area
🌱
Producer — Makes its own food via photosynthesis (grass, trees, algae)
🐇
Consumer — Eats other organisms (rabbit eats grass, hawk eats rabbit)
🍄
Decomposer — Breaks down dead matter, recycles nutrients (fungi, earthworms)
🔗
Food chain — Single pathway: Grass → Rabbit → Fox
🛰
Food web — Many food chains linked together in a network
Food Webs
What If a Species Disappears?

Example: All Frogs Disappear from a Pond

Insects (frog prey) ↑

With no frogs eating them, insect populations explode. They damage crops and plants nearby.

Herons (frog predator) ↓

Herons lose a major food source. Their population declines or they move elsewhere.

Removing one species causes a chain reaction through the whole web.

Food Webs
Invasive Species

An invasive species is a non-native organism introduced to an ecosystem where it has no natural predators.

Real Example: Cane Toads in Australia

Introduced in 1935 to eat beetles on sugar cane farms. With no predators, their population exploded. They produce poison that kills animals trying to eat them, and they outcompete native frogs for food and space.

Three Ways Humans Harm Ecosystems

  1. Deforestation — Clearing rainforests for palm oil destroys orangutan habitats
  2. Pollution — Plastic in oceans is eaten by sea turtles who mistake it for jellyfish
  3. Overfishing — Taking cod faster than they reproduce crashes marine food webs

The Energy Pyramid

At every level, about 90% of energy is lost as heat. Only ~10% passes up.

Energy Flow
Calculating Energy Flow

Worked Example

A field of grass captures 100,000 kJ from the sun.

1
Grasshoppers (primary) get 10% → 10,000 kJ
2
Frogs (secondary) get 10% of that → 1,000 kJ
3
Hawks (tertiary) get 10% of that → 100 kJ

Formula

Energy at next level = Current level × 0.10

Biomass Also Decreases

Biomass = total mass of living organisms at each level. There's far more grass (by mass) than hawks. That's why top predators are rare — not enough energy to support many of them.
